\begin{helper}
For every \(\varepsilon>0\), there is a real number
\(\delta\) with \(0<\delta\le 1\) such that, for every real
\(x\) satisfying \(0<x\le\delta\),
\[
  x\log_2\left(\frac{2e}{x}\right)\le \frac{\varepsilon}{4}.
\]
\end{helper}
\begin{proof}
As \(x\to0^+\), we have \(x\to0\) and \(x\log x\to0\).  Since
\[
  x\log\left(\frac{2e}{x}\right)
  =x\log(2e)-x\log x
\]
for \(x>0\), it follows that
\[
  x\log\left(\frac{2e}{x}\right)\to0
  \qquad (x\to0^+).
\]
Dividing by the positive constant \(\log 2\) gives
\[
  x\log_2\left(\frac{2e}{x}\right)\to0
  \qquad (x\to0^+).
\]
Because \(\varepsilon/4>0\), there is \(\eta>0\) such that the displayed
quantity is \(<\varepsilon/4\) whenever \(0<x<\eta\).  Taking
\(\delta=\min(\eta/2,1)\) gives \(0<\delta\le1\), and every
\(0<x\le\delta\) satisfies \(0<x<\eta\).  Hence the required weak
inequality follows.
\end{proof}
