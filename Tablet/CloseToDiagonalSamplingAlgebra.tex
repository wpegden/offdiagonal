\begin{helper}
Let \(k\ge1\), let \(E,I,F\) be real numbers, and suppose
\[
  I\le \left(\frac{e}{k}\right)^k F,\qquad
  F\le 2^E,\qquad
  1\le I.
\]
Define
\[
  p_0=\frac{k}{e}\,2^{-E/k}.
\]
Then
\[
  p_0^k I\le1
  \qquad\text{and}\qquad
  p_0\le1.
\]
\end{helper}
\begin{proof}
Since \(k\ge1\), all divisions by \(k\) are legitimate and \(p_0>0\).
First compute
\[
  p_0^k
  =
  \left(\frac{k}{e}\right)^k 2^{-E}.
\]
Using the two assumed upper bounds gives
\[
\begin{aligned}
  p_0^k I
  &\le
  \left(\frac{k}{e}\right)^k2^{-E}
  \left(\frac{e}{k}\right)^k F\\
  &\le
  \left(\frac{k}{e}\right)^k
  \left(\frac{e}{k}\right)^k
  2^{-E}2^E\\
  &=1.
\end{aligned}
\]
This proves \(p_0^k I\le1\).  Since \(I\ge1\) and \(p_0^k\ge0\), it follows
that \(p_0^k\le p_0^k I\le1\).  If \(p_0>1\), then \(p_0^k>1\) because
\(k>0\), contradicting \(p_0^k\le1\).  Hence \(p_0\le1\).
\end{proof}
