\begin{theorem}
For any \(s\ge 4\), there is a positive constant \(c_s\) such that for any \(k\ge 2\),
\[
  r(s,k)\ge c_s\frac{k^{s-2}}{(\log k)^{2s-6}}.
\]
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Fix \(s\ge4\), and put
\[
  t=s-2.
\]
Then \(t\ge2\). We first prove the desired lower bound for all sufficiently large \(k\), with all constants allowed to depend on this fixed \(s\).

Let
\[
  H(Q)=100t^2 Q(\log Q)^2.
\]
For every sufficiently large \(k\), choose \(q\) to be the largest power of \(2\) such that \(H(q)\le k/2\). Since the powers of \(2\) are unbounded, such a \(q\) exists for large \(k\); and since
\[
  \frac{H(2q)}{H(q)}
  =2\left(\frac{\log(2q)}{\log q}\right)^2
\]
tends to \(2\), we may require \(k\) large enough that \(H(2q)\le4H(q)\). The maximality of \(q\) gives \(H(2q)>k/2\), hence \(H(q)>k/8\). Therefore
\[
  \frac{k}{8}\le 100t^2 q(\log q)^2\le \frac{k}{2}. \tag{1}
\]
Because \(q\) is a power of \(2\), there is a finite field \(K\) of order \(q\).

Set
\[
  G=G(t,K),\qquad F=\overline G,
\]
where the complement is the loop-graph complement from \(\noderef{LoopGraphComplement}\). Since \(t=s-2\), \(\noderef{ComplementPolarityPairHsFree}\) gives that \((F,G)\) is \(H_s\)-free.

Define
\[
  n=\frac{q^{t+1}-1}{q-1},\qquad
  d_G=\frac{q^t-1}{q-1},\qquad
  a=\frac{q^{t-1}-1}{q-1},\qquad
  \lambda_G=\sqrt{d_G-a}.
\]
By \(\noderef{PolarityGraphParameters}\), \(G\) is an \((n,d_G,\lambda_G)\)-graph in the sense of \(\noderef{LoopGraphNdLambda}\). Since
\[
  n=1+q+\cdots+q^t,\qquad
  d_G=1+q+\cdots+q^{t-1},\qquad
  d_G-a=q^{t-1},
\]
we have, for all sufficiently large \(q\),
\[
  \frac12q^t\le n\le2q^t,\qquad
  \frac12q^{t-1}\le d_G\le2q^{t-1},\qquad
  \lambda_G^2=q^{t-1}. \tag{2}
\]

We now verify the corresponding parameters for \(F\). The complement has the same vertex set by \(\noderef{LoopGraphComplement}\), so \(F\) has \(n\) vertices. Symmetry is inherited: if \(xy\in E(F)\), then \(xy\notin E(G)\); if \(yx\in E(G)\), the \(\noderef{LoopGraphSymmetric}\) conclusion for \(G\) would imply \(xy\in E(G)\), a contradiction, so \(yx\notin E(G)\), hence \(yx\in E(F)\).

For degrees, \(\noderef{LoopGraphDegree}\) counts all adjacent vertices, loops included once. For each vertex \(v\), exactly \(d_G\) of the \(n\) possible vertices \(w\) satisfy \(vw\in E(G)\), and by \(\noderef{LoopGraphComplement}\) exactly the remaining \(n-d_G\) satisfy \(vw\in E(F)\). Thus \(F\) is regular of degree
\[
  d_F=n-d_G=q^t.
\]
In particular, by (2), \(d_F\ge n/2\).

Finally, the non-principal eigenvalue bound for \(F\) is the same as for \(G\). Let \(A_G\) and \(A_F\) denote the adjacency actions from \(\noderef{LoopGraphAdjacencyAction}\). If \(f\) has coordinate sum zero, then for every vertex \(v\),
\[
  A_Ff(v)=\sum_{w:\,vw\in E(F)}f(w)
        =\sum_w f(w)-\sum_{w:\,vw\in E(G)}f(w)
        =-A_Gf(v).
\]
Therefore, if \(\mu\) is a non-principal adjacency eigenvalue of \(F\) in the sense of \(\noderef{LoopGraphNonprincipalEigenvalue}\), then \(-\mu\) is a non-principal adjacency eigenvalue of \(G\). Since \(G\) is an \((n,d_G,\lambda_G)\)-graph, \(|\mu|=|-\mu|\le\lambda_G\). Thus \(F\) is an \((n,d_F,\lambda_F)\)-graph with
\[
  \lambda_F=\lambda_G.
\]

Set
\[
  \eta=\max\left\{
    \frac{\lambda_G^2}{d_G^2},
    \frac{\lambda_F\lambda_G}{d_Fd_G}
  \right\}.
\]
Using (2), \(d_F=q^t\), and \(\lambda_F=\lambda_G\), we get constants depending only on \(s\) such that, for all large \(q\),
\[
  \frac{1}{16q^{t-1}}\le \eta\le\frac{16}{q^{t-1}}. \tag{3}
\]
In particular \(\eta\le1\) for large \(q\). Also \(n\ge q^t/2\), so \(n^2\) grows like \(q^{2t}\), while the lower bound in (3) is a constant multiple of \(q^{-(t-1)}\). Since \(t\ge2\), increasing the large-\(k\) threshold if necessary gives
\[
  n^{-2}\le\eta. \tag{4}
\]
The remaining standing hypotheses of \(\noderef{RamseyFromGraphPair}\), namely \(s,n\ge3\), \(d_G>0\), and \(k\ge1\), also hold for all sufficiently large \(k\).

We next verify the two numerical hypotheses in the second conclusion of \(\noderef{RamseyFromGraphPair}\). First,
\[
  \frac{n(\log n)^2}{d_G}
  =(1+o(1))q\,t^2(\log q)^2,
\]
because \(s\), and hence \(t\), is fixed, while \(n=(1+o(1))q^t\) and \(d_G=(1+o(1))q^{t-1}\). Thus for large \(q\),
\[
  100\frac{n(\log n)^2}{d_G}
  \le 200t^2q(\log q)^2
  \le k, \tag{5}
\]
where the last inequality is the upper bound in (1).

Second, from (2) and (3),
\[
  \eta d_F n
  \ge \frac{1}{16q^{t-1}}\cdot \frac n2\cdot n
  \ge 2^{-7}q^{t+1}. \tag{6}
\]
Since \(t\ge2\), the quantity \(q^{t+1}\) eventually dominates \(q(\log q)^2\). Combining this with the lower bound in (1), we get, for large \(q\),
\[
  k\le 800t^2q(\log q)^2\le \eta d_F n. \tag{7}
\]

All hypotheses of the second conclusion of \(\noderef{RamseyFromGraphPair}\) are now checked: \(F\) and \(G\) are the required \((n,d_F,\lambda_F)\)- and \((n,d_G,\lambda_G)\)-graphs, \((F,G)\) is \(H_s\)-free, (3) and (4) give \(n^{-2}\le\eta\le1\), and (5), (7) give
\[
  100n(\log n)^2/d_G\le k\le \eta d_Fn.
\]
Therefore
\[
  r(s,k)\ge \frac{k}{100\eta}-1. \tag{8}
\]

We now compare this with the claimed expression. The upper bound in (3) gives
\[
  \frac{k}{100\eta}\ge \frac{1}{1600}kq^{t-1}. \tag{9}
\]
From the lower bound in (1),
\[
  k\le 800t^2q(\log q)^2.
\]
For large \(k\), \(q\le k\), so \(\log q\le\log k\). Hence
\[
  q\ge \frac{k}{800t^2(\log k)^2}. \tag{10}
\]
Substituting (10) into (9) yields a positive constant \(C_s\), depending only on \(s\), such that
\[
  \frac{k}{100\eta}
  \ge
  C_s\frac{k^t}{(\log k)^{2t-2}}.
\]
Since \(t=s-2\), this is
\[
  \frac{k}{100\eta}
  \ge
  C_s\frac{k^{s-2}}{(\log k)^{2s-6}}. \tag{11}
\]
After increasing the large-\(k\) threshold one last time, the subtraction of \(1\) in (8) can be absorbed into the right hand side of (11). Thus there are constants \(C'_s>0\) and \(k_0\) such that, for all \(k\ge k_0\),
\[
  r(s,k)\ge C'_s\frac{k^{s-2}}{(\log k)^{2s-6}}. \tag{12}
\]

It remains to include the finitely many integers \(2\le k<k_0\). By \(\noderef{RamseyNumber}\), each \(r(s,k)\) is a positive integer, and \(\log k>0\) for \(k\ge2\). Hence each number
\[
  r(s,k)\frac{(\log k)^{2s-6}}{k^{s-2}}
\]
is positive. The set of such \(k\) is finite, so its minimum is positive when the set is nonempty. Choose \(c_s>0\) to be at most \(C'_s\) and at most that finite minimum. Then (12) handles \(k\ge k_0\), and the choice of \(c_s\) handles \(2\le k<k_0\). This proves the theorem for the fixed \(s\ge4\).
\end{proof}
