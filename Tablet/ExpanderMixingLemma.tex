\begin{lemma}
Let \(G\) be an \((n,d,\lambda)\)-graph, with loops allowed, and let \(A,B\subseteq V(G)\). Then
\[
  \left|e_G(A,B)-\frac d n |A||B|\right|
    \le \lambda\sqrt{|A||B|}.
\]
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
Let \(V=V(G)\). By \(\noderef{LoopGraphNdLambda}\), the graph has \(|V|=n\), is symmetric, every vertex has degree \(d\), and every non-principal adjacency eigenvalue has absolute value at most \(\lambda\). We use the standard inner product on real-valued functions on \(V\),
\[
  \langle f,g\rangle=\sum_{v\in V} f(v)g(v),
\]
with norm \(\|f\|^2=\langle f,f\rangle\). Let \(\mathbf 1\) be the constant-one function, and let \(T=A_G\) be the adjacency action from \(\noderef{LoopGraphAdjacencyAction}\).

If \(n=0\), then \(V\) is empty, so \(A=B=\emptyset\). By \(\noderef{LoopGraphEdgeCountBetween}\), \(e_G(A,B)=0\), and both sides of the desired inequality are \(0\). Thus assume \(n>0\).

We first record the linear-algebra consequences of the \((n,d,\lambda)\)-graph hypotheses. By the degree condition in \(\noderef{LoopGraphNdLambda}\), interpreted through \(\noderef{LoopGraphDegree}\), every row of the adjacency matrix has exactly \(d\) ones, loops included once. Therefore
\[
  T\mathbf 1=d\,\mathbf 1. \tag{1}
\]
By the symmetry condition in \(\noderef{LoopGraphNdLambda}\), interpreted through \(\noderef{LoopGraphSymmetric}\), the adjacency matrix is symmetric, so \(T\) is self-adjoint for the above inner product. Hence the orthogonal complement
\[
  \mathbf 1^\perp=\{f:\sum_{v\in V}f(v)=0\}
\]
is \(T\)-invariant, and the restriction of \(T\) to \(\mathbf 1^\perp\) has an orthonormal eigenbasis. The eigenvalues appearing on this subspace are precisely the non-principal adjacency eigenvalues in the sense of \(\noderef{LoopGraphNonprincipalEigenvalue}\), so \(\noderef{LoopGraphNdLambda}\) gives
\[
  \|Tf\|\le \lambda\|f\|
  \qquad\text{for every }f\in\mathbf 1^\perp. \tag{2}
\]
Here \(\lambda\) is the nonnegative spectral bound in the \((n,d,\lambda)\)-graph convention.

Let \(1_A\) and \(1_B\) be the indicator functions of \(A\) and \(B\). By \(\noderef{LoopGraphAdjacencyAction}\),
\[
  (T1_B)(v)=\sum_{\substack{w\in V\\ vw\in E(G)}}1_B(w).
\]
Therefore
\[
  \langle 1_A,T1_B\rangle
  =
  |\{(a,b)\in A\times B:ab\in E(G)\}|
  =
  e_G(A,B), \tag{3}
\]
where the last equality is exactly \(\noderef{LoopGraphEdgeCountBetween}\). If \(a=b\) and \(aa\in E(G)\), the diagonal adjacency entry contributes once to the sum above and once to the ordered pair count, so loops are handled by the same identity.

Decompose the two indicator functions into their all-ones components and zero-sum parts:
\[
  1_A=\alpha\mathbf 1+f,\qquad
  1_B=\beta\mathbf 1+g,
\]
where
\[
  \alpha=\frac{|A|}{n},\qquad \beta=\frac{|B|}{n}.
\]
Then
\[
  \sum_{v\in V} f(v)=|A|-\alpha n=0,
  \qquad
  \sum_{v\in V} g(v)=|B|-\beta n=0,
\]
so \(f,g\in\mathbf 1^\perp\).

Using (1), self-adjointness of \(T\), and the zero-sum identities for \(f\) and \(g\), we compute
\[
\begin{aligned}
  \langle 1_A,T1_B\rangle
  &=\langle \alpha\mathbf 1+f,\ T(\beta\mathbf 1+g)\rangle\\
  &=\alpha\beta\langle \mathbf 1,T\mathbf 1\rangle
    +\alpha\langle \mathbf 1,Tg\rangle
    +\beta\langle f,T\mathbf 1\rangle
    +\langle f,Tg\rangle\\
  &=\alpha\beta\,d\,\langle\mathbf 1,\mathbf 1\rangle+\langle f,Tg\rangle\\
  &=\frac d n |A||B|+\langle f,Tg\rangle.
\end{aligned} \tag{4}
\]
Indeed, \(\langle\mathbf 1,\mathbf 1\rangle=n\), while
\[
  \langle \mathbf 1,Tg\rangle=\langle T\mathbf 1,g\rangle
  =d\langle\mathbf 1,g\rangle=0
\]
and similarly \(\langle f,T\mathbf 1\rangle=0\).

Combining (3) and (4), it remains to bound \(|\langle f,Tg\rangle|\). By Cauchy's inequality and (2),
\[
  |\langle f,Tg\rangle|
  \le \|f\|\,\|Tg\|
  \le \lambda\|f\|\,\|g\|. \tag{5}
\]
It remains only to estimate the two norms. Since \(1_A\) takes the value \(1\) on \(A\) and \(0\) off \(A\),
\[
\begin{aligned}
  \|f\|^2
  &=\sum_{v\in V}\left(1_A(v)-\frac{|A|}{n}\right)^2\\
  &=|A|-\frac{|A|^2}{n}
  \le |A|.
\end{aligned} \tag{6}
\]
The same calculation gives
\[
  \|g\|^2=|B|-\frac{|B|^2}{n}\le |B|. \tag{7}
\]
Taking square roots in (6) and (7), and substituting into (5), gives
\[
  |\langle f,Tg\rangle|
  \le \lambda\sqrt{|A|}\sqrt{|B|}
  =\lambda\sqrt{|A||B|}.
\]
Using (3) and (4), this is precisely
\[
  \left|e_G(A,B)-\frac d n |A||B|\right|
    \le \lambda\sqrt{|A||B|}.
\]
\end{proof}
