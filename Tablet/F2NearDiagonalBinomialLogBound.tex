\begin{helper}
Let \(n,m,b\in\mathbb N\).  If \(m>0\), \(b>0\), and \(b\le m\), then
\[
  \binom{n}{m}\le
  2^{m\log_2\left(e n/b\right)}.
\]
\end{helper}
\begin{proof}
If \(n=0\), then \(m>0\) gives \(\binom{0}{m}=0\), so the claim is
immediate.  Assume \(n>0\).  The standard binomial estimate
\[
  \binom{n}{m}\le \frac{n^m}{m!}
\]
reduces the problem to bounding \(m!\) from below.  The elementary bound
\[
  \frac{m^m}{m!}\le e^m
\]
is obtained from the exponential series estimate
\[
  \frac{x^m}{m!}\le e^x
\]
by taking \(x=m\).  Since \(m>0\), this is equivalent to
\[
  \frac1{m!}\le \frac{e^m}{m^m}.
\]
Multiplying by \(n^m\) yields
\[
  \binom{n}{m}\le n^m\frac{e^m}{m^m}
  =\left(\frac{en}{m}\right)^m.
\]
Because \(0<b\le m\), we have \(1/m\le1/b\), and hence
\[
  \left(\frac{en}{m}\right)^m
  \le
  \left(\frac{en}{b}\right)^m.
\]
Finally, \(en/b>0\) in the nonzero case, and
\[
  \left(\frac{en}{b}\right)^m
  =
  2^{m\log_2(en/b)}.
\]
Combining the displayed inequalities proves the claim.
\end{proof}
