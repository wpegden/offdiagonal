\begin{helper}
For every \(\varepsilon>0\), there is \(s_0\in\mathbb N\) such that every
integer \(s\ge s_0\) satisfies
\[
  s\le 2^{\varepsilon s/2}.
\]
\end{helper}
\begin{proof}
Put
\[
  c=\frac{\varepsilon\log 2}{2}.
\]
Since \(\varepsilon>0\) and \(\log 2>0\), we have \(c>0\). The standard
exponential growth estimate says that
\[
  \frac{e^x}{x}\to\infty
  \qquad\text{as }x\to\infty.
\]
Applying this with \(x=cs\), which also tends to infinity as \(s\to\infty\),
there is \(s_0\) such that, for all \(s\ge s_0\),
\[
  \frac{e^{cs}}{cs}\ge \frac1c.
\]
Multiplying by the positive number \(cs\) gives \(s\le e^{cs}\). Finally,
\[
  e^{cs}
  =
  e^{(\log 2)(\varepsilon s/2)}
  =
  2^{\varepsilon s/2}.
\]
Thus \(s\le2^{\varepsilon s/2}\) for every \(s\ge s_0\).
\end{proof}
