\begin{helper}
For every real number \(0<\delta<1/10\), there is an integer \(L\) with the
following property.  Let \(s,k,q\) be natural numbers and let
\(\eta,w,Q\) be real numbers.  Put \(x=k/s\).  If \(L\le s\), \(Ls\le k\),
\[
  Q=q^{s-3},\qquad x^{1-\delta/2}\le q,\qquad
  0<\eta\le 1,\qquad \eta\le \frac{16}{Q},\qquad
  w\le \frac{\delta k}{5},
\]
then
\[
  k\le \frac{1}{128}q^{s-1}
\]
and
\[
  x^{(1-\delta)s}\le
  \frac{1}{50}k\,\eta^{(w-k)/k}-1 .
\]
\end{helper}
\begin{proof}
Apply \(\noderef{F2NearDiagonalLargeSPowerBound}\) with
\(\epsilon=\delta/100\), and choose \(L\) also so large that
\(L\ge 1600\), \(L\ge 200/\delta\), and \(L\ge 1000/\delta\).  Then
\(x=k/s\ge L\), so \(x\ge 1600\), and \(s\ge L\).

First prove the range estimate.  Let \(b=\delta s/200\).  Since
\(s\ge 200/\delta\), we have \(b\ge 1\), and since \(x\ge 128\),
\[
  128\le x\le x^b .
\]
The large-power bound gives \(s\le 2^b\), and \(x\ge2\) gives
\(2^b\le x^b\), hence \(s\le x^b\).  Therefore
\[
  128\,s\,x\le x^{1+\delta s/100}.
\]
Because \(\delta<1/10\) and \(s\ge4\),
\[
  1+\delta s/100\le (1-\delta/2)(s-1).
\]
Using \(x^{1-\delta/2}\le q\), we get
\[
  128k=128sx\le x^{(1-\delta/2)(s-1)}\le q^{s-1},
\]
which is \(k\le q^{s-1}/128\).

It remains to prove the Ramsey-comparison estimate.  Put
\(a=1-\delta/2\), \(c=1-\delta/5\), \(E=a(s-3)\), and
\(e=(w-k)/k\).  From \(w\le\delta k/5\) and \(k>0\), we have
\[
  e\le -c .
\]
Also \(\eta Q\le16\), so \(Q/16\le\eta^{-1}\).  Since \(c>0\),
\[
  (Q/16)^c\le \eta^{-c}\le \eta^e .
\]
The hypothesis \(x^a\le q\) and \(Q=q^{s-3}\) imply \(x^E\le Q\).  Since
\(x\ge16\) and \(E\ge1\), this gives
\[
  x^{E-1}\le Q/16,
\]
and hence \(x^{(E-1)c}\le\eta^e\).

Finally, the choice \(s\ge1000/\delta\) makes the exponent gap nonnegative:
\[
  (1-\delta)s\le 1+(E-1)c.
\]
Thus
\[
  x^{(1-\delta)s}\le x^{1+(E-1)c}\le x\,\eta^e.
\]
Since \(s\ge1600\), we have \(2\le s/50\), and therefore
\[
  2x^{(1-\delta)s}\le \frac{s}{50}x\,\eta^e
    =\frac{k}{50}\eta^e .
\]
The left-hand side \(x^{(1-\delta)s}\) is at least \(1\), so
\[
  x^{(1-\delta)s}+1\le 2x^{(1-\delta)s}
    \le \frac{k}{50}\eta^e .
\]
Rearranging gives the claimed inequality.
\end{proof}
